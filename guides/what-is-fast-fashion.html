What is fast fashion?

The business model, the environmental cost and the human cost of fast fashion — explained plainly, without the industry greenwashing.

The definition

Fast fashion is a business model built around producing large volumes of trend-led clothing as quickly and cheaply as possible, moving new styles from runway to retail in weeks rather than months, and encouraging consumers to buy more and discard more frequently. The term was coined in the early 1990s when Zara pioneered a model of 2–3 week design-to-store cycles. Today, some ultra-fast fashion brands produce thousands of new styles weekly.

How the model works

Traditional fashion operated on two seasons per year — spring/summer and autumn/winter. Fast fashion brands operate on 52 micro-seasons or more, with new stock arriving weekly. This creates a sense of constant newness and urgency — if you don't buy now, it will be gone. The model depends on consumers buying more frequently than they need to and discarding garments after a handful of wears.

The cost reductions that make low prices possible come from three places: cheaper materials (predominantly synthetics), lower-cost manufacturing (primarily in Bangladesh, China, Cambodia, India and Vietnam), and reduced quality control. The price consumers pay has been pushed down dramatically over the past 30 years — the average UK household now buys twice as many garments as in the year 2000, at roughly half the price per item.

The environmental cost

The fashion industry produces an estimated 10% of global carbon emissions — more than aviation and shipping combined. Fast fashion's contribution is significant and growing.

  • Water use: It takes approximately 2,700 litres of water to produce a single cotton T-shirt — roughly what one person drinks in 2.5 years. Cotton farming is one of the most water-intensive agricultural activities globally.
  • Chemical pollution: Textile dyeing is the world's second largest polluter of clean water. Many of the chemicals used are toxic and not adequately treated before being discharged into rivers and groundwater.
  • Microplastics: Synthetic fabrics — polyester, nylon, acrylic — shed microplastic fibres with every wash. An estimated 500,000 tonnes of microplastics from textiles enter the ocean annually. These are now found in human blood, placentas and the deepest ocean trenches.
  • Textile waste: The equivalent of a rubbish truck full of textiles is landfilled or incinerated every second globally. The UK alone sends around 300,000 tonnes of clothing to landfill each year.

The human cost

The low prices of fast fashion depend on low wages and poor working conditions in manufacturing countries. Garment workers — predominantly women — in Bangladesh, Cambodia, Vietnam and other major manufacturing countries typically earn poverty wages and work in difficult conditions.

The 2013 Rana Plaza factory collapse in Bangladesh, which killed 1,134 garment workers, brought the human cost of fast fashion to global attention. The building was unsafe and workers had reported cracks in the structure, but were told to continue working. Brands including Primark, Benetton and others were found to be sourcing from factories in the building.

Progress has been made since 2013 — the Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety has improved factory safety standards, and many brands have strengthened supplier auditing. But the fundamental economics of fast fashion create pressure on manufacturers that is difficult to overcome with audit programmes alone.

Greenwashing

Many fast fashion brands have launched sustainability-labelled collections — H&M's Conscious Collection, Zara's Join Life, ASOS's Responsible Edit. These collections typically use a higher proportion of organic or recycled materials and are marketed with sustainability messaging.

However, these collections represent a small fraction of total production. H&M produces over 3 billion garments annually; its Conscious Collection is a rounding error by volume. Regulators in Norway and Denmark have found H&M's environmental claims to be misleading. The Norwegian Consumer Authority determined that the brand's sustainability scorecards were incomplete and could not substantiate the claims made.

What you can do

Buy less, buy better. A single well-made garment that lasts five years has a lower environmental footprint than five cheap garments bought and discarded in the same period. Check fabric labels, prioritise natural fibres, and use FabricMark's brand reviews to understand what you are actually buying.
