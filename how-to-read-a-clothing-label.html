How to read a clothing label

The label sewn into your clothing contains everything you need to assess its quality and longevity — if you know how to read it. Here is what every number and fibre name actually means.

Why clothing labels matter

Fashion marketing is designed to make you feel good about a purchase, not to give you accurate information about what you are buying. The label is different. By law, every garment sold in the UK must state its fibre composition accurately by percentage. It cannot lie.

Understanding what that composition means — whether a garment will last, how it will feel over time, and what its environmental footprint is — is one of the most useful skills you can have as a clothing buyer. It takes thirty seconds once you know what to look for.

The fibre percentage breakdown

The most important part of any clothing label is the fibre composition, usually listed as a percentage breakdown such as “80% cotton, 20% polyester.” These percentages tell you what the fabric is physically made from.

Fibres fall into three broad categories, each with very different properties:

How to read the percentage

The percentage tells you what proportion of the fabric is made from each fibre. The key principle is simple: the higher the proportion of natural fibres, the better the quality and longevity of the garment in almost every case.

A few practical rules of thumb:

  • 100% cotton, 100% wool, 100% linen or 100% silk are generally the best quality choices within their respective price points.
  • Blends that are 70%+ natural fibre are still good. A 70% wool, 30% polyamide coat benefits from the durability the polyamide adds to the wool without sacrificing too much quality.
  • Blends that are 50%+ synthetic are a warning sign. A 60% polyester, 40% cotton T-shirt will feel noticeably cheaper and will not last.
  • Any garment that is 100% polyester, 100% acrylic or predominantly synthetic is low quality regardless of branding or price.

The care label symbols

Alongside fibre content, every label includes care symbols. These are standardised internationally and tell you how to wash, dry, iron and dry-clean the garment safely. Key symbols to know:

  • A tub with a number — machine wash at that temperature in degrees Celsius. A crossed-out tub means do not machine wash.
  • A circle — dry clean. A crossed-out circle means do not dry clean.
  • A square with a circle — tumble dry. A crossed-out version means lay flat to dry.
  • An iron shape with dots — iron at the temperature indicated by dots (one dot = cool, three dots = hot). Crossed out means do not iron.
  • A hand in water — hand wash only. Never machine wash this garment.

Country of manufacture

UK law requires every garment to state its country of manufacture. This tells you where the labour that made your clothing came from, not where the brand is headquartered. A garment sold by a British brand may be manufactured in Bangladesh, Cambodia or Turkey. Country of manufacture alone does not tell you about labour conditions, but it is useful context when assessing a brand’s ethics.

FabricMark rule of thumb

Before buying any garment, take 30 seconds to check the label. If it is 50% or more synthetic, ask yourself whether you need it or whether something better quality at the same price is available. Often it is.

What to look for in store

Beyond the label, there are physical checks you can do before buying that will tell you a lot about quality:

  • The touch test — does the fabric feel substantial, smooth and soft? Or thin, scratchy and flimsy? Your hands are a good quality detector.
  • Check the seams — seams should be even, closely spaced and lying flat. Sloppy, loose or overlapping stitching is a sign of poor construction.
  • Look for pilling — if a garment is already pilling or showing loose fibres on the hanger, it will look worse after one wash.
  • Check linings — a well-made garment has full, well-constructed linings. A poor-quality one has minimal or absent lining.
  • Buttons and zips — pull the buttons gently. They should feel secure. Zips should run smoothly without catching.
